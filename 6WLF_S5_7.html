Conclusions

  • In the northern Colombian andes, which is characterized by geological and tectonic diversity, and tropical environment, landslide occurrence is not an stochastic process. Landslide tends to occur in very especific areas.

  • Well-known geomorphologic parameters, such as hypsometric index and longitudinal river profiles, indicate a close relationship between landslides and channel network.

  • $\chi$ index has been applied to stablish landslide occurrence in the context of hillslope-channel coupling under a dynamic landscape evolution.

  • Finally, it is fundamental to unravel landslide occurrence and landscape evolution. This will permits to evaluate landslide occurrence under a dynamic context of climate change and land-use changes by anthropogenic causes.

River profile analysis

The change in channel bed elevation at any point along the longitudinal profile with respect to time (dz/dt) reflects a competition between the rates of rock uplift and channel incision with respect to base level.

$\frac{dz(x,t)}{dt}=U(x,t)-E$

where U is the rock uplift rate and E erosion rate. If E is change by $q_s$ and $S$ by $dz/dx$ then:

$\frac{dz(x,t)}{dt}=U(x,t)-K(x,t)A^m(\frac{dz(x,t)}{dx})^n$

Under steady-state conditions, a perfect balance between rock uplift and incision results in time-invariant topography ($dz/dt=0$).

$\frac{dz}{dx}=(\frac{U}{KA^m})^{1/n}$

$\chi$ index

Perron & Royden (2012) perform the integration in the upstream direction from a base level $x_b$ to an observation point $x$ yields an equation for the elevation profile. If U and K are uniform, they can be brought outside the integral, and:

$z(x)=z(x_b)+(\frac{U}{K})^{1/n}\int_{x_b}^x\frac{dx}{A(x)^{m/n}} $
$z(x)=z(x_b)+(\frac{U}{KA_0^m})^{1/n} \chi$

where the longitudinal coordinate is equal to:

$\chi=\int_{x_b}^x(\frac{A_0}{A(x)})^\theta dx$

and $A_0$ is an arbitrary scaling area that gives $\chi$ units of length. $\chi$ depends only upon the modern distribution of drainage area, not on topography.
